Rock n' roll died in 1958...

And came back five years later...

...with a British accent.

In 1963,

America was desperate to experience something new and exciting. Meanwhile, a group of four Liverpudlians were taking Britain by storm.

In the aftermath of John F. Kennedy’s assassination, the future looked bleak and joyless. In 1963, various American radio DJs tuned into the new trends in Britain, and began playing early Beatles songs like “Please Please Me”. Their popularity quickly escalated in the U.S., and when The Beatles landed in New York in February 1964, thousands of fans gathered in the airport to meet them.

The Stones

The Stones arrived to America in June 1964. Their bluesy, rock n’ roll attitude captivated tons of rebellious teens.

The band started out playing the blues, along with covers of old rock n' roll songs by the artists like Chuck Berry and Buddy Holly. The group landed at JFK Airport in June 1964 to find over 500 fans awaiting their arrival. They were marketed as rebels, with long hair and surly personalities. Each member had a very distinct stage presence: Mick Jagger danced and leapt about on stage, Keith Richards shuffled forwards and back, and Brian Jones posed and preened for the girls. The Stones were proud of their personality and made sure to let everyone know it.

The Second Wave

As rock music progressed in the 60s, British bands moved away from Merseybeat and toward a sound influenced by the tastes of Mods and Rockers.

American teens could appreciate The Kinks' deep criticisms of modern society and The Who's fiery onstage energy. The second wave of British bands to come to America hit the scene in the mid-1960s. Mods and Rockers constantly clashed, with bands following suit. Some of these bands tended to be badly behaved compared to the first wave, smashing instruments onstage and causing a general ruckus.

The Mod Takeover

Mod culture was full of clean red, white, and blue graphics, high necklines and Mary Quant clothes, and sincere hatred for the Rockers, their very opposites.

Diana Rigg

The Avengers was the first British-made TV series to be brought to America in 1966. Diana Rigg played the clever, sly agent with excellent martial arts skills, rarely needing rescuing by the show's male lead, played by Patrick Macnee. She quickly became a star in America for her quick wit and feminist values.

Twiggy

This skinny supermodel took America by storm, changing the world of fashion with her unique short-haired, androgynous look. Across America, young women cropped their hair, painted on longer lower lashes, and wore minidresses to copy the Twiggy look. After remaining at the top of her profession for four years, she retired and moved onto movies and records.

The Kinks

Famous for their blues-revival-turned-Mod tunes, The Kinks didn't care a lot about invading America. Formed in 1962, their sometimes angsty, sometimes critical music spurred a widespread following in America.

The group was made up of brothers Ray and Dave Davies, Peter Quaife, and Mick Avory. Many of their songs took a dig at current trends (like the London Mods, who happened to be dedicated fans) and American customs, after a tour to America full of fights with promoters and even each other. Many of their songs were very clearly English, (sung with an English accent about English customs such as afternoon tea), especially compared to other British Invasion bands, who sang about love and happiness.

Vocals

Roger was a skiffle guitarist with his own band, The Detours, when he met John Entwistle in 1962. After Entwistle as well as Pete Townshend joined the band, Daltrey fired the group's lead singer and became both lead guitarist and lead vocalist, and they later became The Who with the addition of Keith Moon. Daltrey demonstrated great vocal range in many of The Who's songs. He later went on to a fairly successful solo career in both music and acting.

THE WHO

John Entwistle

Bass

Trumpeter John got together with Pete Townshend to form a traditional jazz band called 'The Scorpions' in 1959. Entwistle decided to pick up the guitar when he heard the twangy guitar sound of Duane Eddy, but later set it aside to play bass when Townshend began playing guitar. He later joined The Detours as a bassist. He was nicknamed "The Ox" based on the way his fingers blurred across the heavy bass's fretboard. Entwistle is widely regarded as one of the greatest bassists who ever lived.

Keith Moon

Drums

After meeting The Who in 1963, Keith Moon, at that point part of a different band, asked for a chance to sit in with The Who. At this informal audition, Moon performed with such enthusiasm that he broke the bass drum pedal. The band was impressed by his aggressive playing and immediately asked him to join them. Later on, the onstage destruction of instruments would become part of their act. In 1978, Moon died of a drug overdose after a long battle with addiction.

Cream

Though they only lasted from 1966 to 1968, Cream had an enormous influence on all the heavy-metal bands that followed in their wake. The lineup consisted of guitarist Eric Clapton from The Yardbirds, bass player Jack Bruce, and drummer Ginger Baker. Their powerhouse, improvisational heavy-rock sound appealed to a mass audience across the states.

Influence on American Culture

"It was like being in the eye of a hurricane. You'd wake up in a concert and think, Wow, how did I get here?" - John Lennon

Once British acts got a foothold in America, they never really went away. They brought in long hair, Carnaby street fashions, and transformed the world of pop merchandising. The British saw the American promise of a free and colorful land, and helped introduce parts of American culture that had gone unnoticed, like the R&B of the north and the rich blues of the South. It's doubtfulthat the JFK Airport will ever see the teenage fan mania witnessed on February 9, 1964, the day The Beatles landed in America.

Who Was Influenced?

In their success, British groups in America paved the way for American blues, garage, and folk-rock bands playing nonpop rock to move to national success.

The Monkees

The Monkees, made up of four hand-selected actors and musicians, sprouted up from a TV show about a goofy rock band modeled after The Beatles. The TV show, full of laughs and catchy hits, was an immediate success in America, mildly rivaling America's admiration for The Beatles.

George Harrison's 12-string Rickenbacker guitar was especially inspiring, becoming a signature instrument for The Byrds (most notably in their hit song, below).

There was one band who decided to re-enact the American Revolution and take a stand against the British invaders. Paul Revere & The Invaders looked and sounded similar to British Invasion bands, with a part-Beatles, part-Stones, and part-Animals sound, but they dressed in American Revolutionary War soldier costumes as a gimmick (they actually were a pretty great rock group behind the costumes, though).

The British Are Coming... Again!

The 1960s British Invasion wasn't the last time America would be hearing from Great Britain.

British acts proved to be worth listening to over and over again. In the 1980s, America saw a rush of popular British acts, primarily due to the newly-surfaced MTV. Mainly synthpop and new wave acts dominated, with top singles by bands like The Human League and A Flock of Seagulls. In the 1990s and 2000s, even more British groups stole American hearts, with bands such as Coldplay, Muse, and The Kaiser Chiefs topping the charts.

Formed in Manchester in 1982, The Smiths have been referred to as the most important alternative rock band to emerge from the British independent music scene of the 1980s. Their sound was a fusion of 1960s rock and post-punk. Morissey, lead vocalist, created a highly unique sound with his unusual baritone (and sometimes falsetto) style.

The Clash

As an integral part of British punk, The Clash had a far-reaching influence on alternative rock. Their politically-charged lyrics, experimental music, and rebellious attitude was highly appealing to a wide range of fans. Their music weaved in elements of punk, reggae, dub, funk, and rockabilly.
